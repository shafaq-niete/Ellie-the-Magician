# 3 Tips for Constructive, Growth-Focused Teacher Observation Feedback

Practical, well-sourced tips for writing feedback that helps a teacher grow after a classroom observation.

## Tip 1 — Anchor feedback in specific, observed evidence (not vague impressions)
Point to exact moments from the lesson — a question the teacher asked, a phrase students used, how a task was scaffolded — and tie the teacher's action to the student outcome it produced. Specific, evidence-based comments feel fair and actionable; generalizations feel like judgment. Balance this by naming a genuine strength first so the growth area lands as support rather than criticism.
- Source: [Providing Teachers With Feedback That Fosters Growth — Edutopia](https://www.edutopia.org/article/providing-teachers-feedback-fosters-growth)
- Source: [20 Constructive Teacher Observation Feedback Examples — ClickView](https://www.clickvieweducation.com/blog/teaching-ideas/teacher-observation-feedback)

## Tip 2 — Deliver it promptly, ideally within 24 hours
Give feedback close in time to the observation, while the lesson is still fresh for both of you. Timely feedback keeps examples concrete and the conversation relevant, which makes the teacher far more likely to act on it.
- Source: [Providing Teachers With Feedback That Fosters Growth — Edutopia](https://www.edutopia.org/article/providing-teachers-feedback-fosters-growth)
- Source: [8 Teacher Observation Feedback Examples — Kuraplan](https://www.kuraplan.com/blog/teacher-observation-feedback-examples)

## Tip 3 — Agree on ONE clear, actionable next step, not a long checklist
Instructional-coaching research warns against overwhelming teachers with a list of fixes. Instead, focus on one highly specific element of practice and collaboratively agree on a single change the teacher can implement right away — offered as an option to try rather than a directive. One well-supported step drives more real improvement than ten vague ones.

# Practical Answers for Common Teaching Problems

A short, research-backed guide of concrete strategies teachers can try right away, organised by four common problem areas.

> **Note on "CRM":** The abbreviation is ambiguous. In this guide it is interpreted as **Classroom Routines & Management** (behaviour). If a different meaning was intended (e.g. Customer/Client Relationship Management software), this section should be re-scoped.

---

## 1. CRM — Classroom Routines & Management (behaviour)

**The problem:** Lost teaching time, low-level disruption, and inconsistent behaviour when expectations and routines are unclear.

**What the research says:** Effective, evidence-based management rests on maximising structure and predictable routines, teaching and reinforcing a small number of clear expectations, and responding calmly and consistently to behaviour (PBIS frameworks; IES; Edutopia).

**Try tomorrow:**
1. **Post 3-5 positive expectations and explicitly teach them.** Keep them short, phrased as what to *do* ("We listen when others speak"), and review them regularly rather than assuming students know them.
2. **Over-rehearse your key routines.** Model a routine (entering, transitions, packing up) and have the class practise it three or more times until each step is automatic. This front-loading prevents most disruption later.
3. **Correct quietly and briefly.** Handle off-task behaviour on the spot without drama, using a low-key cue such as "What should you be doing right now?", and pair it with specific praise when students get it right.

## 2. Classroom Culture

**The problem:** Students feel disconnected, unsafe to take risks, or not valued, which undermines both wellbeing and achievement.

**What the research says:** Strong teacher-student relationships, a sense of belonging, and social-emotional learning are the foundation of a positive culture, and are linked to better academic performance and fewer behaviour incidents.

**Try tomorrow:**
1. **Greet every student by name at the door.** A warm, personal greeting has been linked to better self-regulation and higher class participation.
2. **Use the "Two-by-Ten" strategy for a hard-to-reach student.** Spend two minutes a day, for ten days, in low-stakes conversation getting to know one student. It is a well-known relationship-building move associated with better rapport and fewer disruptions.
3. **Build in short SEL moments.** Brief check-ins or naming-and-managing-emotions activities help students feel seen and create a more inclusive, respectful room.

## 3. Feedback (to students)

**The problem:** Feedback is often too vague, arrives too late, or focuses only on grades, so it doesn't actually move learning forward.

**What the research says:** Hattie and Timperley's influential model says effective feedback answers three questions: **"Where am I going?"** (the goal), **"How am I going?"** (current progress), and **"Where to next?"** (the next step). High-information feedback aimed at the *process* and *self-regulation* is far more powerful than praise, grades, or simple right/wrong marks.

**Try tomorrow:**
1. **Tie feedback to a clear goal.** Before commenting, restate the success criteria so the student knows what "good" looks like, then comment against it.
2. **Always give a "where to next" step.** End feedback with one specific, actionable improvement the student can act on immediately, rather than only judging the finished work.
3. **Comment on strategy, not just the answer.** Point to *how* the student approached the task ("Try checking your working at each step") to build process-level and self-regulatory skills, which drive deeper learning.

## 4. Perseverance / Grit / Growth Mindset

**The problem:** Students give up when work gets hard, treat mistakes as proof they "aren't smart," and avoid challenge.

**What the research says:** Carol Dweck's research shows that in a growth mindset, difficulty is seen as a natural part of learning, so students persist and look for better strategies. A growth mindset can be taught, and it is linked to greater grit (perseverance), drawing on the work of Duckworth and of Yeager and Dweck.

**Try tomorrow:**
1. **Praise effort and strategy, not ability.** Say "You kept trying different approaches" rather than "You're so smart," so students attribute success to things they control.
2. **Use the word "yet."** Reframe "I can't do this" as "I can't do this *yet*," making struggle feel temporary and normal.
3. **Treat mistakes as data.** Openly analyse errors as useful information ("What can this mistake teach us?") so students learn to take difficulty in their stride instead of feeling inadequate.
